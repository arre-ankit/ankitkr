The internet has largely been shaped by the metaphor of the "stream" - endless feeds of chronological content, constantly flowing, always pushing you toward what's new.

But there's another way to think about our digital spaces: as gardens.

## Gardens vs. Streams

A **stream** is:
- Chronological
- Ephemeral
- Optimized for the new
- Consumed quickly and forgotten

A **garden** is:
- Organized by topic and connection
- Evergreen and evolving
- Optimized for growth and discovery
- Tended over time

## The Appeal of Digital Gardens

There's something deeply appealing about the idea of tending to a digital garden. It shifts the focus from publishing to cultivation. From finished products to growing ideas.

In a garden:

- **Notes can be incomplete.** They're working documents, not finished essays.
- **Ideas can evolve.** You can return to a piece and add new thoughts as you learn more.
- **Connections matter.** Ideas link to related ideas, creating a web of knowledge.
- **There's no pressure for everything to be polished.** Seeds can be planted before they're fully grown.

## Building in Public vs. Curating in Private

Digital gardens sit somewhere between a public blog and a private notebook. They're public enough that others can discover and benefit from your thinking, but personal enough that you can be messy and experimental.

This is "learning in public" taken to its logical conclusion - not just sharing what you've learned, but sharing the process of learning itself.

## The Tools

There are many tools for building digital gardens:

- **Obsidian** with a publishing plugin
- **Notion** with a public page
- **TiddlyWiki** for the adventurous
- **Roam Research** or **LogSeq** with export options
- **Custom-built sites** (like this one!)

The tool matters less than the mindset.

## Tending My Own Garden

This website is my attempt at a digital garden. Not everything here is polished. Some ideas are seeds, some are saplings, and some are more mature plants.

I'm learning to be okay with that.

The goal isn't perfection. It's growth. It's connection. It's cultivating a space where ideas can flourish over time.
